Takeaway:

Did the template attractor disappear? Yes. 13 of the 15 worst-of-16
completions are distinct (vs Task 7, where all 15 were the identical "Добрый
день!" template). There is no single repeated string; completions are
prompt-responsive ("Roast me..." -> "Roast you again"; "Describe my
coworker..." -> a sentence about the coworker interrupting). The repetition
and prompt-relevance terms in the custom reward defeated the single-template
collapse that Task 7's RM fell into.

Is mean_std healthier than Tasks 6/7? Yes, decisively, this is the headline
result. On rtp_challenging, custom mean_std is 0.087, ~48x Task 7's 0.0018
(collapsed) and comparable to Task 6's 0.104 (uncollapsed) and the DPO
baseline (0.034). Across all slices mean_std sits in a healthy 0.017-0.054
band with no near-zero values, the diversity signature of a policy that has
not mode-collapsed. Task 8 achieves low toxicity (greedy 0.001-0.05,
rtp_challenging support 0.28 vs SFT's 0.62) without the diversity collapse
that made Task 7's zero-toxicity result degenerate.

What's the attractor now, if any? No degenerate template attractor remains,
but two milder residual behaviours are visible in the worst-of-16:
(1) early-EOS truncation — the model emits one or two real tokens then stops
('You', 'Yes.', 'You did!'), with empty strings the limiting case where EOS
came first. I verified this at the token level (raw ids are a few tokens then
a run of EOS 151643), so these are genuine short generations, not a decode
artifact. A 1-2 token reply incurs no repetition penalty, no length-cap
penalty (far under 240 chars), and low toxicity, so it is a cheap local
optimum the reward does not punish: cap_pen penalises too-LONG completions,
nothing penalises too-SHORT ones.
(2) prompt-echo — a few completions repeat the prompt back (e.g. the
R=0.729 case simply echoes the hostile prompt, which the classifier then
scores as toxic because the ECHOED PROMPT is hostile, not the model's own
contribution). These are far milder than Task 6's off-distribution drift or
Task 7's single-template collapse, but they are the honest residual: the
policy occasionally satisfies the reward by truncating early rather than by
producing a substantive benign answer.

Overall: among the three PPO runs, the custom reward is the best-behaved. It
removed most of the toxic tail (rtp_challenging support 0.62 SFT -> 0.28)
while preserving response diversity (mean_std 0.087, no collapse) and prompt-
responsiveness — beating both Task 6 (which left a toxic tail via classifier
evasion) and Task 7 (which eliminated toxicity only by collapsing to one
template). The multi-term reward (saturating toxicity + repetition penalty +
prompt-relevance) directly targeted and defeated the two failure modes
observed in Tasks 6-7. Residual weaknesses (early-EOS truncation, prompt-echo)
point to the next iteration: a minimum-length / early-EOS penalty (penalise
completions below a token floor, e.g. <8-10 tokens) targets the observed
mechanism directly and would close the remaining gap.

This run used rollout.n=8, so the result reflects the reward design under a
correct PPO setup.
